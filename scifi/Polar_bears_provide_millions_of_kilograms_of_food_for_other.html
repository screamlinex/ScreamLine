Science / October 28, 2025

Polar bears provide millions of kilograms of food for other Arctic species

Summary: A groundbreaking study quantifies the staggering amount of carrion left by polar bears—over 5 million kilograms per season—providing a vital food source for Arctic scavengers. As polar bear populations dwindle, the cascading effects on species like Arctic foxes, lemmings, and gulls could reshape the region’s entire ecosystem.

Food Chain Contributions

Polar bears, the apex predators of the high Arctic, consume vast amounts of seals each year. When they leave behind carcasses after hunting, these remains become a key nutrient input for scavengers. The study shows that approximately 70 % of the prey biomass is accessed by other species, supporting populations that rely on opportunistic feeding.

Scavenger Dependency

Arctic foxes, snowy owls, and various gull species have adapted to feast on polar bear leftovers. These carrion events boost breeding success and increase survival rates during the harsh winter months. Loss of this food source could trigger population declines, especially in species already pressured by habitat loss and climate change.

Impact of Polar Bear Decline

With polar bear numbers projected to shrink by up to 40 % by 2050, the study predicts a corresponding drop in carrion availability of up to 2 million kilograms annually. This reduction could cascade into reduced body condition in scavengers, altering predator-prey dynamics and potentially opening niches for invasive species.

Research Methodology

The team combined satellite tracking, field observations, and isotopic analysis to estimate carrion production. By tagging 120 bears across three Arctic regions, they mapped kill sites and monitored scavenger usage patterns, providing the first comprehensive dataset on polar bear-derived food webs.

Conservation Implications

These findings underscore the need for holistic Arctic conservation strategies that account for indirect trophic interactions. Protecting polar bear habitats not only preserves the bears themselves but also safeguards the broader ecological network that depends on their carcasses for survival.
