Science / October 29, 2025

Mercury shines farthest from the sun in the evening sky on Oct. 29

Summary: Mercury appears as a bright “evening star” in the southwestern sky at sunset on Oct. 29, its maximum elongation from the Sun making it a rare viewing opportunity.

Visibility Details

On Oct. 29 Mercury will rise just after sunset, reaching an angular separation of 28° from the Sun—the planet’s greatest elongation for the year. The bright planet will be visible for roughly 2½ hours after sunset before fading into twilight. It will appear as a sharp, silver-white point roughly 0.3° above the horizon, just south of the constellation Orion.

Why It Happens

Mercury orbits the Sun every 88 days, but its inclined orbit and proximity to the Sun mean it is rarely visible at great angles. When the planet reaches its greatest elongation, its orbit aligns so that we see the side of the planet that is fully illuminated by the Sun, creating a brilliant evening star. This event occurs about twice a year, making Oct. 29 a prime opportunity for observers.

Observing Tips

Use a small telescope or binoculars with an 8‑10× magnification to spot Mercury’s crescent phase. Avoid bright artificial lights and find a dark sky near a horizon line free of obstructions. The planet will appear slightly bluish-white; a steady hand and a quick scan of the southwestern twilight will help you locate it before it disappears into the light.

Scientific Interest

Mercury’s visibility at greatest elongation allows amateur astronomers to measure its angular distance from the Sun with a small telescope, refining orbital parameters used by planetary scientists. It also provides a natural laboratory for studying Mercury’s surface composition and reflectivity through photometric observations, aiding models of its volatile loss and magnetic field generation.
