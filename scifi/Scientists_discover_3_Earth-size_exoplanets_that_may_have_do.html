Science / October 27, 2025

Scientists discover 3 Earth-size exoplanets that may have double sunsets — like Tatooine in Star Wars

Summary: NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) has uncovered a remarkable trio of Earth-sized planets orbiting a tight binary star system, a configuration that challenges conventional models of planetary formation. The three worlds, each about the size of our own, line up in a resonant chain that may produce dramatic “double sunsets” when viewed from their surfaces, echoing the iconic Tatooine of the Star Wars saga.

Discovery & the TESS Mission

Scientists first spotted the faint dip in starlight that hinted at these planets during TESS’s second year of observations. After rigorous vetting and confirmation with ground‑based follow‑up, the team identified three transiting bodies in a 1:2:3 orbital resonance. The data revealed orbital periods of roughly 8, 16, and 24 days, a pattern that is both rare and scientifically valuable for testing theories of planetary migration.

A Tight Binary Star System

The host system consists of two sun‑like stars locked in a 4‑day orbit, separated by only about 0.03 astronomical units. Their combined luminosity and close proximity create a dynamic gravitational environment that would normally destabilize planetary orbits. Yet the planets maintain stable, coplanar paths thanks to their synchronized resonance, a phenomenon that scientists are still working to fully explain.

Planetary Dynamics & Double Sunsets

Because the stars eclipse each other every few days, each planet experiences alternating phases of daylight from one star and then the other. Depending on the orbital phase, observers on a planet’s surface could witness a sunset that simultaneously lights up two distinct suns, producing a “double sunset” effect that would be both a breathtaking visual and a challenge for any future habitability assessments.

Implications & Future Observations

These findings suggest that tightly packed, resonant planetary systems around binary stars may be more common than previously thought. Upcoming missions like the James Webb Space Telescope and ground‑based extremely large telescopes will probe the atmospheres of these worlds for signs of weather patterns and potential biosignatures. Meanwhile, dynamical simulations are refining our understanding of how such exotic configurations can remain stable over billions of years.
