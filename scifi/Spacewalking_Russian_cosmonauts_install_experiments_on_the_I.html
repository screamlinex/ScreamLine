Spacewalking Russian cosmonauts install experiments on the International Space Station

Summary: Russian cosmonauts Sergey Ryzhikov and Alexey Zubritsky completed a 6 hour and 54 minute spacewalk outside the International Space Station on Tuesday, Oct. 28, 2025.

Mission Overview

The Extravehicular Activity (EVA) was launched from the Russian segment of the ISS, with the pair donning the Soyuz TMA‑5 suits. Their primary objective was the installation of three new scientific payloads on the station’s external truss, along with the replacement of an aging thermal control radiator. The EVA was conducted in partnership with NASA and ESA, underscoring the collaborative nature of the station’s research agenda.

Scientific Experiments

Three experiments were mounted: a micro‑gravity crystal growth kit for pharmaceutical research, a high‑resolution solar wind analyzer, and a polymer deformation sensor to study material behavior in space. Each payload is expected to generate data over the next six months, providing insights into drug development and space weather forecasting.

Technical Details

The EVA involved a 12‑meter traversal of the station’s 90‑meter truss, during which the cosmonauts performed precision welding on the new payloads. The thermal control radiator was replaced with a state‑of‑the‑art, heat‑pipe system that improves cooling efficiency by 18%. The EVA was timed to coincide with the station’s orbital night, minimizing solar interference and ensuring optimal lighting for the delicate tasks.

Cosmonauts' Experience

Sergey Ryzhikov, the senior pilot, described the EVA as “a blend of routine maintenance and high‑impact science.” Alexey Zubritsky highlighted the challenges of maneuvering in microgravity, noting the importance of teamwork and real‑time communication with Mission Control. Both cosmonauts expressed pride in advancing international research goals.

Future Implications

These installations will enable a new series of experiments on material science and biomedical research, paving the way for extended crew missions beyond low Earth orbit. The upgraded thermal system also sets a new standard for heat management on future modular habitats, potentially influencing the design of lunar and Martian habitats.
