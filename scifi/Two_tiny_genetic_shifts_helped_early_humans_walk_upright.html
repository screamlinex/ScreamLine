Two tiny genetic shifts helped early humans walk upright

Summary: Scientists have linked bipedalism to changes in how the human pelvis developed millions of years ago. Two subtle genetic shifts in the regulatory regions of key bone‑forming genes—*SHANK3* and *COL1A1*—are now believed to have nudged the pelvis toward a narrower, more upright shape, paving the way for efficient walking and running in early hominins.

The Pelvic Puzzle

The pelvis is a living archive of our evolutionary history. Its arch, width, and curvature determine not only how we sit and move, but also how our hips support the weight of an upright torso. Paleontologists have long noted that early hominins like *Ardipithecus ramidus* and *Australopithecus afarensis* had pelvises that were narrower and more bowl‑shaped than those of modern humans, a subtle shift that dramatically altered locomotion.

First Genetic Shift: *SHANK3* Regulatory Variant

Recent comparative genomics identified a single nucleotide change in an enhancer region upstream of the *SHANK3* gene. Although *SHANK3* is best known for its role in synaptic scaffolding, the enhancer variant modulates its expression in mesenchymal cells that form pelvic bones. In vitro assays showed that the human variant increases *SHANK3* transcription by 12%, leading to tighter regulation of osteoblast activity and a slight reduction in the breadth of the iliac blades.

Second Genetic Shift: *COL1A1* Promoter Deletion

The second key alteration is a 9‑base pair deletion in the promoter of *COL1A1*, the gene encoding type I collagen, the main structural protein of bone. This deletion reduces promoter activity by roughly 20%, slowing the rate of collagen deposition during pelvic development. The result is a more slender, mechanically efficient pelvis that better supports vertical loading while still providing a stable base for the upper body.

Implications for Human Evolution

These two tiny genetic tweaks—each affecting gene expression by less than a quarter—collectively shifted pelvic geometry enough to facilitate bipedal walking. The changes likely conferred advantages in energy efficiency and stability, enabling early hominins to traverse open savannahs, hunt, and carry objects. By integrating genetic, fossil, and biomechanical data, researchers now have a clearer picture of how seemingly minor genomic variations can ripple into major behavioral innovations.
