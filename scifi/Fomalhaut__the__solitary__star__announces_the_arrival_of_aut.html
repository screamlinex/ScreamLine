Fomalhaut, the 'solitary' star, announces the arrival of autumn in the night sky. Here's how to see it

Summary: If there is one star that serves to announce the arrival of fall as the leaves have begun to turn, unquestionably that title would have to go to Fomalhaut.

Fomalhaut: The Southern Autumn Herald

Fomalhaut (Alpha Piscis Austrini) is the brightest star in the constellation Piscis Austrinus, lying just above the celestial equator. Its spectral type, A4V, makes it a blue-white dwarf that shines with a luminosity roughly 16 times that of the Sun. Historically, ancient cultures noted its rising in late September as a herald of the harvest season, and modern astronomers now recognize it as a reliable marker for the start of autumn in the Southern Hemisphere.

Why Fomalhaut Marks the Change of Seasons

Fomalhaut's heliacal rising—its first appearance above the eastern horizon after a period of invisibility—coincides with the equinox in the Southern Hemisphere. When the Sun dips below the horizon at sunset, Fomalhaut climbs steadily across the sky, reaching its highest point around midnight in late September and early October. This timing aligns with the peak of the fall foliage cycle in many temperate regions, making the star a natural seasonal cue.

How to Find Fomalhaut in the Night Sky

Locate the Southern Cross (Crux) first; Fomalhaut lies roughly 10 degrees south of the Cross’s tip. From the Cross’s southernmost star, follow a straight line outward, and you will spot the bright point of light that is Fomalhaut. It is easily distinguishable by its sharp, white hue against the darker backdrop of the Milky Way. If you’re in the Northern Hemisphere, you can still spot Fomalhaut on clear evenings, but it will appear lower in the sky and may be obscured by light pollution.

Practical Tips for Observing

Plan your observation for a moonless night when the sky is darkest; Fomalhaut’s magnitude 1.16 makes it visible even in moderate light pollution, but a darker sky enhances its contrast. Use a small telescope or binoculars to appreciate its subtle color variations, and consider photographing the star with a DSLR set to long exposure to capture the surrounding star field. Finally, pair your observation with a star chart app that highlights Fomalhaut’s position relative to the constellations for a richer stargazing experience.
