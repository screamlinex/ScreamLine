Black holes are encircled by thin rings of light. This physicist wants to see one

Summary: Theoretical physicist Alex Lupsasca is pushing for a space telescope to glimpse the thin ring of light that is thought to surround every black hole.

The Ring of Light

When light passes close to a black hole, its path is bent by the extreme gravity, creating a narrow, bright ring known as the photon sphere. According to General Relativity, this ring is a universal feature of all black holes, regardless of their size or spin. Detecting it would provide a direct visual confirmation of the theory’s predictions.

Why It Matters

Observing the photon ring would allow astronomers to measure black hole properties—mass, spin, and even potential deviations from Einstein’s equations—with unprecedented precision. It would also help resolve the “information paradox” by offering clues about how light and matter behave near the event horizon.

The Proposed Telescope

Lupsasca’s team proposes a dedicated, high‑resolution infrared observatory equipped with a coronagraph to block out the bright central source. The instrument would operate from a high Earth orbit, providing the stability and angular resolution needed to resolve micro‑arcsecond structures around nearby supermassive black holes.

Technical and Budgetary Challenges

Building such a telescope requires advances in adaptive optics, detector sensitivity, and precise spacecraft pointing. Funding agencies have expressed concerns about the cost, estimated at $1.2 billion, and the long development timeline of roughly a decade.

Community Response

While many in the astrophysics community applaud the ambition, some caution that the Event Horizon Telescope’s recent image of M87* already demonstrates the feasibility of ring‑like features. Still, the new mission would push the limits further, potentially capturing the ring in multiple black holes and opening a new era of strong‑field tests.
