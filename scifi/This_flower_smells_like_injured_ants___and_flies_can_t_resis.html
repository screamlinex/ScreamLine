This flower smells like injured ants — and flies can’t resist it

Summary: A type of Japanese dogsbane releases a scent identical to wounded ants’ distress signal, drawing in scavenging flies that unwittingly pollinate it.

The Flower and Its Habitat

Endemic to the misty valleys of Kyushu, the Japanese dogsbane (Lycium japonicum) thrives in well-drained volcanic soils and open woodlands. Its pale pink blossoms, each just a few millimeters across, open in early spring, a time when many pollinators are scarce. The plant’s survival strategy relies on a chemical trick that mimics the chemical alarm signals of injured ants, a rare adaptation among flowering plants.

The Ant Scent Mechanism

Chemical analysis has identified a blend of alkylpyrazines and (E)-2-hexenal in the dogsbane’s nectar that is chemically indistinguishable from the volatile compounds ants release when injured. These compounds trigger an innate response in flies, compelling them to land on the flower as if it were a food source or a site where wounded ants congregate.

Fly Interaction and Pollination

Common house flies (Musca domestica) and several species of stable flies (Stomoxys calcitrans) are the primary visitors. While they seek the “ant scent,” their bodies pick up pollen grains from the flower’s reproductive organs. When they move on to other flowers or resting spots, they transfer pollen, completing the plant’s reproductive cycle without the flies realizing they are pollinators.

Ecological Implications

This mimicry highlights a sophisticated evolutionary arms race. By exploiting the chemical communication of ants, the dogsbane reduces competition for pollinators and increases its chances of successful reproduction. It also illustrates how non-plant organisms can influence plant pollination networks, a factor that may become increasingly important as ecosystems shift with climate change.

Future Research Directions

Scientists are now exploring whether other plant species employ similar chemical tricks and how widespread this strategy is in the plant kingdom. Additionally, researchers aim to determine the genetic basis for the production of these alkylpyrazines, which could shed light on the evolution of plant–insect communication pathways.
