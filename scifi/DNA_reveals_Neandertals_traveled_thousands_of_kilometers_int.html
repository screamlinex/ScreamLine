Science / October 27, 2025

DNA reveals Neandertals traveled thousands of kilometers into Asia

Summary: DNA and stone tool comparisons suggest Eastern European Neandertals trekked 3,000 kilometers to Siberia, where they left a genetic and cultural mark.

The Genetic Journey

Using high‑coverage genome sequencing of Neandertal remains from Croatia and the Ussuri region of Russia, researchers identified a shared haplotype that traces back to a single migratory wave. The genetic markers indicate a rapid eastward expansion, challenging the long‑standing view that Neandertals were confined to Western Eurasia.

Stone Tools and Cultural Exchange

Comparative analysis of flint knapping styles shows a remarkable convergence between the Upper Palaeolithic toolkits of the Balkans and those found in the Amur basin. The presence of the “Ushkovsky” blade‑making technique, previously documented only in Eastern Europe, in Siberian sites points to direct contact or migration rather than independent invention.

Implications for Neandertal Migration

These findings suggest that Neandertals were far more mobile than previously assumed, capable of traversing diverse landscapes—from temperate forests to tundra—within a few generations. The genetic imprint on Siberian populations also hints at possible admixture events, adding a new layer to the complex mosaic of human ancestry in the region.

Methodology and Analysis

The study combined radiocarbon dating, isotopic profiling of bone collagen, and Bayesian phylogenetic modeling. By integrating archaeological context with genomic data, scientists achieved a robust chronology that places the migration event at roughly 42,000 years ago, coinciding with climatic shifts that may have opened new migratory corridors.

Future Directions

Ongoing projects aim to sequence additional Neandertal genomes from the Caucasus and Central Asia to map the full extent of their eastward movements. Moreover, interdisciplinary collaborations with climate scientists will help reconstruct the environmental conditions that facilitated such extensive travels.
