Napoleon’s retreating army may have been plagued by these microbes

Summary: DNA extracted from the molars of soldiers buried in the Grande Armée’s mass graves revealed two potent, fever‑causing bacteria—Yersinia pestis, the plague pathogen, and Streptococcus pyogenes, the agent of streptococcal pharyngitis and toxic shock. Their simultaneous presence may have amplified the already disastrous conditions of Napoleon’s 1812 Russian retreat, contributing to the high mortality rate among the troops.

The Discovery

In a collaborative effort between microbiologists and historians, researchers analyzed dental calculus and enamel fragments from 57 soldiers recovered near the Berezina River. Using next‑generation sequencing, they identified bacterial DNA fragments that match modern strains of Y. pestis and S. pyogenes. The findings, published in Nature Communications, suggest that these microbes were circulating among the army long before the infamous winter freeze.

The Bacteria

Yersinia pestis is best known as the causative agent of the Black Death, while Streptococcus pyogenes can trigger severe respiratory and skin infections. Both bacteria thrive in crowded, unsanitary conditions—exactly what the Grande Armée endured during its march through Russia. The genetic signatures indicate that the strains were capable of causing acute, systemic infections, potentially leading to high fevers and rapid deterioration in soldiers already weakened by exhaustion and malnutrition.

Impact on the Retreat

Historians have long attributed the army’s collapse to logistical failures, harsh weather, and Russian scorched‑earth tactics. The new microbial evidence adds a biological dimension: widespread febrile illness would have sapped morale, slowed movement, and increased vulnerability to enemy attacks. The timing of the bacteria’s prevalence—peaking during the winter of 1812—coincides with the most catastrophic losses, suggesting a possible causal link.

Scientific and Historical Implications

These findings demonstrate the power of ancient DNA in reconstructing the epidemiology of historical events. They also raise questions about how disease shaped the outcome of military campaigns. Future research aims to map the spread of these pathogens across Europe in the early 19th century and to assess whether similar bacterial outbreaks influenced other historical retreats.
