Can we find water ice on the moon? Only if we know where to look, scientists say

Summary: Space agencies around the world are planning moon missions that will depend on water ice for resources, but much remains unknown about its availability of water ice on the lunar surface.

Recent Discoveries of Polar Ice

Data from NASA’s Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) and the Indian Space Research Organisation’s Chandrayaan‑2 have revealed spectral signatures consistent with water ice in permanently shadowed craters at the Moon’s north and south poles. The ice appears concentrated in localized deposits that could be up to several meters thick, suggesting that the Moon has retained significant amounts of water since the Late Heavy Bombardment. However, the precise distribution, purity, and stability of these deposits are still being mapped.

Technical Challenges in Detection

Detecting ice on the Moon requires a combination of remote sensing techniques: neutron spectroscopy, radar backscatter, and infrared spectroscopy. Each method has its own limitations—neutron detectors can be influenced by hydrogen-rich regolith, radar signals can be confounded by rough surface topography, and infrared signatures can be masked by regolith temperature variations. Additionally, the extreme temperature swings in permanently shadowed regions make ground‑based confirmation difficult, pushing the need for in‑situ landers equipped with drilling and mass spectrometry.

Implications for Future Lunar Missions

The presence of water ice is a cornerstone for sustainable human presence on the Moon. Water can be electrolyzed to produce oxygen for breathing and rocket propellant, and it can be mixed with regolith to create construction materials. NASA’s Artemis program and ESA’s Lunar Gateway plan to harvest ice as a resource, while private companies like SpaceX envision a lunar supply chain that includes “water mining.” Accurate mapping of ice deposits will thus influence landing site selection, habitat design, and mission timelines.

Strategies for Targeted Exploration

Scientists are now advocating for a “search‑and‑target” approach: first, high‑resolution orbital surveys to identify the most promising ice‑rich craters, followed by autonomous landers that can drill, sample, and return data. Advances in autonomous navigation and robotic drilling will allow missions to operate in the harsh polar environments with minimal human oversight. International collaboration, such as joint missions between NASA, ESA, and JAXA, will be crucial to share data, technology, and cost, accelerating the transition from discovery to exploitation.
