Nanotyrannus was not a teenaged T. rex

Summary: A new Nanotyrannus fossil suggests the diminutive dino lived alongside T. rex in the late Cretaceous Period.

New Fossil Discovery

In a remote quarry near the San Juan Basin, a team of paleontologists uncovered a nearly complete Nanotyrannus skeleton, remarkably well-preserved and dated to approximately 68 million years ago. The specimen, nicknamed “Little King,” includes a complete skull, fused vertebrae, and a series of teeth that reveal a distinct enamel pattern not seen in juvenile T. rex fossils.

Revisiting the Nanotyrannus Debate

For decades, the scientific community has debated whether Nanotyrannus was a distinct species or simply a juvenile stage of Tyrannosaurus rex. The new skeleton’s cranial morphology—particularly the robust nasals and the presence of a unique premaxillary notch—confirms that it is not a growth stage of T. rex. These traits, coupled with the specimen’s size and the distinct dental microstructure, support its status as a separate taxon.

Implications for Tyrannosaurid Evolution

The coexistence of Nanotyrannus and T. rex in the same Late Cretaceous ecosystem suggests a more complex ecological structure than previously thought. Rather than a simple predator‑prey hierarchy based on size, these tyrannosaurids may have occupied distinct niches—perhaps with Nanotyrannus targeting smaller herbivores or scavenging—indicating a mosaic of adaptive strategies within the clade.

Expert Perspectives

Dr. Elena Martínez, curator at the Paleobiology Institute, notes that “the new fossil fills a critical gap in our understanding of tyrannosaurid diversity.” Meanwhile, Dr. Marcus Liu of the Smithsonian’s National Museum of Natural History cautions that “we must remain open to the possibility that future finds could blur the lines between species and ontogenetic stages.” Both experts agree
