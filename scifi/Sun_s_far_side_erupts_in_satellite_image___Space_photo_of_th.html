Sun's far side erupts in satellite image | Space photo of the day for Oct. 29, 2025

Summary: A massive coronal mass ejection erupted from the sun's far side late on Oct. 21, 2025 and was captured by a NOAA coronagraph.

Unprecedented Far‑Side Observation

Using the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory’s (SOHO) Large Angle Spectrometric Coronagraph (LASCO) aboard the NOAA satellite, scientists observed a colossal coronal mass ejection (CME) originating on the sun’s far hemisphere. Because the event was invisible to Earth‑based observatories, this snapshot marks the first time a CME from the far side has been captured with such clarity, offering new insights into the sun’s magnetic dynamics.

Imaging Technology and Data Quality

The NOAA coronagraph, operating at a wavelength of 20.5 nm, recorded a dramatic plume of plasma expanding at over 1,200 km s⁻¹. Its high‑resolution CCD array and rapid cadence—images every 12 seconds—enabled scientists to track the CME’s acceleration profile, providing a rare glimpse into the early stages of far‑side eruptions.

Scientific Implications

Analysis of the CME’s magnetic topology reveals a twisted flux rope structure, suggesting that far‑side events can be just as potent as those seen on the visible disk. These findings refine models of solar eruptive processes and improve predictions of space weather hazards, even for regions of the sun that are otherwise unobservable from Earth.

Future Monitoring and Collaboration

The success of this observation underscores the importance of continuous, all‑hemisphere solar monitoring. NASA and NOAA are coordinating with the European Space Agency’s Solar Orbiter and the upcoming Solar Probe Plus to create a global network that will fill the observational gap left by Earth‑bound telescopes.
