Scientists use James Webb Space Telescope to make 1st 3D map of exoplanet — and it's so hot, it rips apart water

Summary: Scientists have leveraged the James Webb Space Telescope to create the first three‑dimensional map of the ultra‑hot Jupiter WASP‑18b. The data reveal a scorching hotspot so intense that it tears apart water molecules, offering unprecedented insight into the planet’s atmospheric dynamics and chemistry.

JWST’s Spectroscopic Breakthrough

Using JWST’s powerful near‑infrared spectrometer, astronomers captured the planet’s light as it transited its host star. By analyzing subtle absorption features across different wavelengths, they reconstructed temperature and composition variations across the planet’s surface, achieving a level of detail previously unattainable for exoplanets.

Three‑Dimensional Atmospheric Mapping

Combining time‑resolved spectra with sophisticated atmospheric models, the team produced a 3D temperature map of WASP‑18b. The map shows a stark day‑side hotspot exceeding 3,000 K, while the night side remains far cooler, creating a dramatic temperature gradient that drives vigorous atmospheric circulation.

Water Molecule Disintegration

At the heart of the hotspot, temperatures surpass the dissociation point of water. Spectral signatures indicate that H₂O molecules break apart into hydrogen and oxygen, a process that dramatically alters the planet’s chemical balance and offers a direct probe of high‑temperature atmospheric physics.

Implications for Hot Jupiter Research

This first 3D map demonstrates how JWST can transform our understanding of exoplanet atmospheres, especially for ultra‑hot Jupiters where extreme conditions reshape chemistry and dynamics. The findings will guide future observations and refine models of planetary formation and evolution.
