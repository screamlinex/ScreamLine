These simple knife tricks stop onion tears instantly

Summary: With a high-speed camera and a tiny guillotine, scientists showed that chopping onions slowly and with sharper knives cuts down on tears.

The Chemistry of Onion Tears

When you cut an onion, the cell walls rupture and release the enzyme alliinase, which converts amino acid sulfoxides into sulfenic acids. These acids rapidly rearrange into syn-propanethial-S‑oxide, the volatile compound that irritates the lacrimal glands, triggering tear production.

High‑Speed Camera Reveals the Process

Researchers filmed the slicing process at 10,000 frames per second, capturing the instant formation and dispersion of the tear‑inducing gas. The footage showed that the gas is released in a narrow plume immediately after each cut, underscoring the importance of knife speed and angle.

Sharper Knives Cut Less

By using a blade with a sharper edge, the knife slices through onion cells with less force, producing smaller fragments and reducing the amount of enzymatic reaction. The study found that a 20‑degree sharper blade cut the tear‑gas output by nearly 40 % compared to a dull blade.

Slow Chopping Saves Eyes

Contrary to the common belief that faster chopping is better, the data indicate that a controlled, slower rhythm allows the gas to disperse before it reaches the eyes. Cutting at a rate of 30 cuts per minute versus 90 cuts per minute decreased tear volume by half.

Practical Tips for Home Cooks

To keep tears at bay, keep your knife razor‑sharp, chop onions at a moderate pace, and consider chilling the onions beforehand to slow the enzymatic reactions. Additionally, cutting the root end last and using a shallow cut near the stem can help minimize gas release.
