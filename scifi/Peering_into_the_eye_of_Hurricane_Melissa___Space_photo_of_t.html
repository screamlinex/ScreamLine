Peering into the eye of Hurricane Melissa | Space photo of the day for Oct. 30, 2025

Summary: Europe's Copernicus Sentinel‑2 satellite has captured unprecedented high‑resolution images of the eye of Hurricane Melissa, offering scientists a new window into the storm’s inner dynamics and potential impacts.

Sentinel‑2: A European Eye on the Skies

Launched in 2015, the Sentinel‑2 constellation comprises two identical satellites—Sentinel‑2A and Sentinel‑2B—each equipped with a 13‑band multispectral imager. With a spatial resolution ranging from 10 m to 60 m, the system provides detailed optical imagery across visible to near‑infrared wavelengths. The latest pass over the Atlantic, timed to coincide with Hurricane Melissa’s peak intensity, produced a series of high‑definition frames that reveal the storm’s eye in unprecedented detail.

Hurricane Melissa: A Record‑Setting Storm

Formed near the Azores on October 27, Melissa rapidly intensified to a Category 4 hurricane, with sustained winds exceeding 260 km/h (160 mph). Its eye—approximately 35 km (22 mi) in diameter—offered a rare opportunity for scientists to study the core structure of a mature tropical cyclone, especially as Melissa’s path threatened the eastern seaboard of the United States.

Unveiling the Eye: What the Images Show

The Sentinel‑2 imagery reveals a sharply defined eye wall with a distinct ring of convective bands. The high‑resolution spectral data highlight temperature gradients, water vapor concentrations, and aerosol plumes that are otherwise invisible to conventional radar. Analysts have identified a subtle “dry patch” within the eye, suggesting an anomalous subsidence pattern that may influence rainfall distribution once the hurricane makes landfall.

Implications for Forecasting and Climate Research

Integrating Sentinel‑2 data into operational weather models has improved the accuracy of wind‑speed and pressure estimates by up to 15 %. The ability to monitor the eye’s structure in near real‑time also aids in assessing the storm’s potential to maintain strength over land. Long‑term, these observations contribute to refining our understanding of tropical cyclone intensity limits under a warming climate.
