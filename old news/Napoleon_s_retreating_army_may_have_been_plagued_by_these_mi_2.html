Napoleon’s retreating army may have been plagued by these microbes

Summary: DNA extracted from the molars of soldiers in Napoleon’s Grande Armée uncovered two fever‑causing bacteria—Yersinia pestis, the plague pathogen, and Streptococcus pyogenes, a common cause of streptococcal pharyngitis and necrotizing fasciitis. These microbes likely amplified the already catastrophic losses during the 1812 Russian campaign, turning a brutal march into a deadly fever epidemic.

Ancient DNA Reveals Hidden Pathogens

Scientists used high‑throughput sequencing on dentine samples from 19th‑century soldiers buried in the Russian steppe. The preserved DNA fragments matched modern genomes of Y. pestis and S. pyogenes with high confidence, confirming that these pathogens were present in the army’s ranks. The study demonstrates that even routine dental tissues can act as time capsules, preserving microbial signatures that survived centuries of burial and decay.

Yersinia pestis and Streptococcus pyogenes: A Deadly Duo

Y. pestis, known for its role in the Black Death, can cause septicemic or pneumonic plague, leading to fever, chills, and rapid organ failure. S. pyogenes, on the other hand, can trigger severe pharyngitis, toxic shock, or necrotizing soft‑tissue infections. In the cramped, cold encampments of the retreating Grande Armée, close quarters and weakened immunity would have facilitated airborne and contact transmission, creating a perfect storm of infectious disease.

Rewriting the Narrative of the Russian Retreat

Traditional accounts emphasize logistical failure, harsh weather, and Russian tactics as the primary causes of the army’s collapse. The discovery of these bacteria suggests that disease was an equally lethal factor, exacerbating the physical toll of the march and accelerating mortality. Understanding the microbiological dimension of the campaign offers fresh insights into how epidemics can undermine military operations, a lesson that resonates with modern armed forces facing biothreats.
