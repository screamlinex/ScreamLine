Napoleon’s retreating army may have been plagued by these microbes

Summary: DNA extracted from the teeth of Napoleonic soldiers revealed two fever‑causing bacteria—Yersinia pestis, the plague pathogen, and Bacillus anthracis, the anthrax agent—potentially exacerbating the catastrophic losses during the 1812 Russian retreat.

The Discovery

Using cutting‑edge ancient DNA protocols, a team of microbiologists analyzed dental pulp samples recovered from mass graves near Smolensk. High‑throughput sequencing and stringent contamination controls allowed the researchers to reconstruct near‑complete genomes of two notorious pathogens, confirming their presence in soldiers who perished during the disastrous campaign.

Identified Microbes

The bacterial signatures matched modern strains of Yersinia pestis, responsible for bubonic plague, and Bacillus anthracis, the causative agent of anthrax. Both organisms are known to trigger high fevers, systemic inflammation, and rapid deterioration—symptoms that would have compounded the soldiers’ already severe exposure to cold, starvation, and disease.

Impact on the Retreat

Historical accounts describe feverish outbreaks and sudden deaths among the Grande Armée. The coexistence of plague and anthrax in the same cohort suggests a compounded infection burden, likely shortening soldiers’ immune resilience and accelerating mortality. This dual infection model helps explain the staggering attrition rates that turned Napoleon’s advance into a rout.

Implications
