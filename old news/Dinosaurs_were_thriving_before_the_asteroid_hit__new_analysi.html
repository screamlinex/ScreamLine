Science / October 23, 2025

Dinosaurs were thriving before the asteroid hit, new analysis suggests

Summary: New dating of New Mexico rocks reveals a vibrant dinosaur fauna thriving just before the Chicxulub impact, challenging the long‑held belief that dinosaurs were already in decline when the asteroid struck.

New Geological Evidence

Geologists at the University of New Mexico have re‑dated a sequence of Permian–Triassic boundary strata in the San Francisco Basin using high‑precision U‑Pb zircon geochronology. The resulting dates place the last dinosaur fossils in the area at 66.2 ± 0.3 Ma, a mere 0.1 million years before the Chicxulub impact. The fossil assemblage, comprising at least five distinct theropod and sauropod taxa, indicates a high level of ecological diversity in a region previously thought to be in ecological decline.

Methodology

The team employed a multi‑disciplinary approach, combining stratigraphic correlation, palynological analysis, and geochemical proxies. U‑Pb dating of detrital zircons yielded a robust age framework, while palynology confirmed a sudden shift from gymnosperm‑dominated to angiosperm‑rich vegetation, suggesting a rapid post‑impact ecological recovery. Radiocarbon calibration was used to cross‑check the age of organic residues associated with the dinosaur remains.

Implications for Mass Extinction

These findings suggest that the dinosaur population was not in a pre‑existing state of collapse, as previously assumed. Instead, the data support a model in which the asteroid impact caused an abrupt, catastrophic extinction event rather than a gradual decline. This challenges the “pre‑impact decline” hypothesis that has been used to explain the relative scarcity of Late Cretaceous fossils in some regions.

Expert Opinions

Dr. Elena García, a paleontologist at the Smithsonian, remarked, “This study forces us to rethink the timing and drivers of dinosaur extinction. The evidence points to a sudden, global shock rather than a slow fade.” Meanwhile, Dr. Thomas Lee, a geochronologist at MIT, noted that “the precision of the U‑Pb dates is unprecedented for this interval, providing a new benchmark for future studies.”
