Most women get uterine fibroids. This researcher wants to know why

Summary: Biomedical engineer Erika Moore is delving into the high prevalence of uterine fibroids among women of color, seeking to uncover the underlying biological and social mechanisms that drive this health disparity.

Background: A Widespread but Under‑Researched Condition

Uterine fibroids—benign tumors that grow in the muscular wall of the uterus—affect up to 70% of women by age 50. Yet the condition is largely invisible in public health discussions, especially for Black, Indigenous, and other women of color who experience higher rates, earlier onset, and more severe symptoms. This gap in understanding fuels disparities in diagnosis, treatment, and outcomes.

Erika Moore’s Engineering Lens

Dr. Erika Moore, a biomedical engineer at the University of California, San Diego, brings a systems‑engineering perspective to the problem. By integrating patient‑reported data, imaging, and molecular profiling, she constructs predictive models that map how genetic, hormonal, and environmental factors converge to promote fibroid growth in specific populations.
