Guppies fall for a classic optical illusion. Doves, usually, do too

Summary: Comparing animals’ susceptibility to optical illnesses can show how perception evolved.

The Classic Illusion

Researchers have long used the Müller‑Lyer illusion—two horizontal lines of equal length flanked by arrowheads pointing in opposite directions—to probe visual perception across species. In humans, the line with inward arrowheads appears shorter, while in many animals the effect is weaker or absent. The new study extends this classic test to guppies and doves, revealing surprising parallels with human perception.

Guppies' Response

In a controlled tank experiment, guppies were presented with pairs of lines that differed only in the orientation of the arrowheads. Remarkably, a majority of the fish consistently chose the line that appeared longer, mirroring the human bias. This suggests that the underlying neural circuitry for processing angular cues may be conserved across vertebrates, even in species with vastly different ecological pressures.

Dove Behavior

Parallel tests with doves—birds known for their acute vision—showed an even stronger illusion effect. When presented with the same line pairs, doves preferred the longer-looking line in over 70% of trials, a rate comparable to that of human participants. The consistency across fish and birds hints at a deep evolutionary root for this perceptual shortcut, possibly linked to the need to rapidly assess distances and shapes in natural environments.

Implications for Evolutionary Biology

These findings support the hypothesis that certain visual heuristics are shared across taxa, reflecting common ancestral neural mechanisms. The fact that both guppies and doves exhibit the illusion indicates that the bias may confer adaptive advantages, such as efficient depth estimation or threat detection, that have been maintained through natural selection. Future work will explore whether this susceptibility varies with ecological niche or developmental stage, offering further insight into the evolution of perception.
