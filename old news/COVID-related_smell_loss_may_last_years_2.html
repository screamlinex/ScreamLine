Science / October 21, 2025

COVID-related smell loss may last years

Summary: Using a scratch-and-sniff test, researchers discovered that smell loss after COVID-19 may linger for more than two years.

Study Design

The international team followed 1,200 participants who had recovered from COVID-19 for up to 30 months. Participants completed a validated scratch-and-sniff odor identification test at multiple time points, allowing researchers to track changes in olfactory function over time.

Key Findings

Approximately 12% of participants continued to score below normal on the odor test after two years, indicating persistent anosmia or hyposmia. The persistence was strongest in those who had severe respiratory symptoms during their acute infection, suggesting a link between viral load, inflammation, and long-term olfactory damage.

Implications for Public Health

These results highlight that COVID-19 can cause lasting sensory deficits, which may impact nutrition, safety (e.g., detecting smoke or gas leaks), and mental health. Health agencies may need to incorporate routine olfactory screening into post-COVID care protocols.

Future Directions

Researchers are now exploring regenerative therapies, such as olfactory training and stem‑cell‑based interventions, to accelerate recovery. Longitudinal studies will also assess whether vaccination status or newer variants influence the duration of smell loss.
