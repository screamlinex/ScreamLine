Science / October 24, 2025

Brain cancer can dissolve parts of the skull

Summary: Glioblastoma, the most aggressive primary brain tumor, is now known to extend beyond the brain tissue, eroding the skull bones and reshaping the immune environment within the skull marrow.

The Unexpected Reach of Glioblastoma

For decades, glioblastoma was treated as a strictly intracranial disease. Recent imaging and surgical observations, however, reveal that the tumor often invades the adjacent cranial bones. This bone invasion is not merely a secondary effect of pressure; it is a direct, active process driven by the tumor’s secretion of osteolytic factors.

Bone Erosion: How the Tumor Invades the Skull

Studies using high-resolution CT and histopathology show that glioblastoma cells infiltrate the diploic layer of the skull, releasing cytokines such as RANKL and IL-6 that stimulate osteoclastogenesis. The resulting bone resorption creates irregular defects that can compromise structural integrity and complicate surgical resection.

Immune System in the Skulls: A New Perspective

The skull marrow hosts a unique population of immune cells, including a high density of resident macrophages and T cells. Tumor infiltration alters this microenvironment, skewing macrophages toward a pro-tumorigenic M2 phenotype and reducing cytotoxic T cell activity. This immunological shift may provide the tumor with a protective niche, shielding it from systemic therapies.

Implications for Diagnosis and Treatment

Recognizing skull bone involvement can improve surgical planning, allowing surgeons to address bone defects simultaneously and reduce postoperative complications. Moreover, targeting bone resorption pathways—such as using bisphosphonates or RANKL inhibitors—could complement conventional therapies, while immunomodulatory strategies might restore a more hostile environment for the tumor.
