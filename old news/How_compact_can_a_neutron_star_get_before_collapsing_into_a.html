How compact can a neutron star get before collapsing into a black hole?

Summary: A recent theoretical study refines the maximum compactness a neutron star can attain before turning into a black hole, tightening constraints on the dense-matter equation of state and offering a novel probe of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at supranuclear densities.

Theoretical Limits on Compactness

Neutron stars are supported against gravity by a balance of quantum degeneracy pressure and strong-force interactions among neutrons, protons, and possibly exotic particles. General relativity imposes a fundamental bound on how compact such an object can be: for a given mass, the radius must exceed the Schwarzschild radius by a factor that depends on the stiffness of the equation of state (EOS). If the EOS is too soft—i.e., pressure rises slowly with density—the star cannot resist collapse, and the critical compactness is reached at a maximum mass of roughly 2–3 M☉.

New Analysis and Its Findings

The study combines data from the NICER X‑ray timing mission, recent gravitational‑wave detections (GW170817 and its electromagnetic counterpart), and advanced many‑body QCD calculations. By fitting the mass–radius relation to a parametric EOS that respects causality and nuclear saturation constraints, the authors find a maximum neutron‑star mass of 2.32 ± 0.05 M☉ and a corresponding radius of 11.4 ± 0.4 km for a 1.4 M☉ star. The most compact configurations approach a dimensionless compactness (GM/Rc²) of 0.30, only 10 % shy of the Buchdahl limit (0.333).

Implications for Quantum Chromodynamics

At densities above ~2–3 ρ₀ (where ρ₀ is nuclear saturation density), QCD predicts a transition from hadronic matter to deconfined quark‑gluon plasma. The refined compactness limits constrain the pressure of this phase and the possible presence of color‑superconducting gaps. If the EOS remains stiff enough to support 2.3 M☉, the data suggest that repulsive vector interactions among quarks—or a strong first‑order transition—must be at play, providing an empirical window into QCD in a regime inaccessible to terrestrial experiments.

Future Observational Tests

Upcoming missions such as the enhanced X‑ray Timing and Polarization (eXTP) satellite and the next‑generation gravitational‑wave detectors (LISA and Einstein Telescope) will refine mass–radius measurements to <10 % precision. Simultaneous observations of tidal deformability in binary mergers and surface emission spectra will tighten the EOS, potentially distinguishing between hadronic and hybrid star models. A confirmed detection of a neutron star with mass > 2.4 M☉ or a radius < 10 km would challenge current QCD-based EOSs and force a re‑evaluation of the strong‑interaction physics at play.
