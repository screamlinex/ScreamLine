A conference just tested AI agents’ ability to do science

Summary: AI promises to speed up scientific analysis and writing. However, AI agents struggled with accuracy and judgment.

Conference Overview

The International Symposium on Artificial Intelligence in Scientific Research (IASIR) gathered leading AI researchers, data scientists, and domain experts to evaluate the capabilities of autonomous agents in performing core scientific tasks. The event featured live demonstrations, peer‑reviewed studies, and a competitive challenge where participants deployed AI agents to design experiments, analyze data, and draft manuscripts.

Methodology

Participants were provided with a curated dataset of 1,200 peer‑reviewed papers across biology, chemistry, and physics. Each AI agent was tasked with (1) generating a hypothesis, (2) proposing a minimal experimental protocol, (3) simulating data, and (4) writing a concise abstract. Human experts then evaluated the outputs on accuracy, reproducibility, and scientific soundness.

Results

While the agents quickly produced polished abstracts, detailed scrutiny revealed frequent logical inconsistencies, citation errors, and over‑generalizations. On average, only 42% of the hypotheses were experimentally viable, and 18% of the proposed protocols contained flawed assumptions. The agents’ language models excelled at mimicking academic tone but lacked the critical judgment required for hypothesis generation.

Implications for the Future

These findings underscore that AI remains a powerful drafting tool but not yet a fully autonomous scientist. Researchers suggest hybrid workflows where AI handles routine data processing and initial drafting, while human scientists maintain oversight of experimental design and interpretation. Continued refinement of reinforcement learning frameworks and domain‑specific knowledge bases may bridge the gap between speed and rigor.
