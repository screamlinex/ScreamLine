Science / October 23, 2025

Dinosaurs were thriving before the asteroid hit, new analysis suggests

Summary: New dating of New Mexico rocks suggest diverse dinosaurs thrived there just before the impact, countering the idea dinos were already on their way out.

New Dating Techniques Uncover Precise Timeline

Using high-precision uranium‑lead and argon‑argon methods, geologists have narrowed the age of the sedimentary layers in the San Juan Basin to within 0.2 million years of the Chicxulub impact. This refined chronology shows a flourishing ecosystem that existed only weeks before the asteroid strike.

Evidence of a Rich Dinosaur Fauna

Fossil assemblages include large theropods, hadrosaurs, and early ceratopsians, along with a diverse array of smaller ornithischians and sauropods. Isotopic analysis of tooth enamel indicates a varied diet, suggesting these species were well-adapted to the region’s ecosystems.

Implications for the Mass Extinction Narrative

These findings challenge the long-held view that dinosaurs were already in decline before the asteroid. Instead, the data suggest a sudden, catastrophic event wiped out a thriving community, reshaping our understanding of the end‑Triassic–early Cretaceous transition.

Future Research Directions

Scientists plan to expand sampling across the Southwestern United States and employ synchrotron imaging to reconstruct finer details of dinosaur physiology. Integrating paleontological data with climate models may reveal how rapid environmental change contributed to the mass extinction.
