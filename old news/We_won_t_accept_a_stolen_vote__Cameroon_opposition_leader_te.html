Global News / October 24, 2025

We won't accept a stolen vote, Cameroon opposition leader tells BBC

Summary: Issa Tchiroma Bakary, leader of Cameroon's main opposition party, declared he had won the presidential election, vowed to reject any claims of fraud, and stated he remains unfazed by potential arrests or imprisonment.

Election Victory Claims

During a live interview with the BBC, Tchiroma Bakary asserted that his party secured a clear majority in the recent polls. He cited independent observers and satellite data as evidence that the vote count was accurate, and called the government's announcement of a victory "a political sham." The opposition leader urged supporters to maintain calm while he prepared to challenge the results in court.

Government Response

President Paul Biya’s administration dismissed the opposition’s allegations, labeling them as “unfounded rhetoric” designed to destabilise the state. Security forces were deployed around key polling stations, and the police announced a crackdown on any “unlawful” protests. Tchiroma warned that such measures would only deepen public mistrust and risk civil unrest.

International Reaction

Western diplomats and human‑rights NGOs expressed concern over the alleged suppression of dissent. The European Union called for an independent audit of the electoral process, while the United Nations urged both sides to engage in dialogue. Tchiroma’s statements have drawn support from several African leaders who see Cameroon’s political climate as a test case for democratic resilience.

Implications for Cameroon

Experts warn that the escalating tensions could lead to a prolonged political standoff. If the opposition’s claims are not addressed, the country risks a repeat of the 2018 crisis that saw widespread protests and a temporary breakdown of public services. Tchiroma’s calm posture, however, may help prevent a violent confrontation, but the upcoming legal battles will likely define Cameroon’s democratic trajectory for years to come.
