‘Elvis’ Producer Schuyler Weiss on Baz Luhrmann’s ‘Joan of Arc,’ Balancing Art and Commerce, and Karaoke With Hugh Jackman

Summary: Schuyler Weiss, the creative force behind “Elvis” and a seasoned producer, took the stage at the Tokyo International Film Festival’s TIFFCOM market to demystify the producer’s role. In a lively MPA masterclass, he likened himself to “a sort of cartoon sheepdog,” relentlessly steering projects forward while maintaining a positive energy. Weiss also shared insights into his collaboration with Baz Luhrmann on “Joan of Arc,” the delicate balance between artistic vision and commercial viability, and a memorable karaoke session with Hugh Jackman that underscored the camaraderie in Hollywood.

Who Is Schuyler Weiss?

Born in the heart of New York’s indie circuit, Weiss quickly rose from script supervisor to A‑list producer, earning acclaim for his work on “The Shape of Water” and the Academy Award‑winning “Elvis.” His career is defined by a blend of sharp business acumen and a deep respect for the storytelling craft. Weiss’s unique approach—treating a film set as a flock of sheep guided by a spirited shepherd—has become a hallmark of his productions.

Mastering the Producer’s Role at TIFFCOM

During his MPA masterclass, Weiss unpacked the day‑to‑day responsibilities that often go unseen by audiences: securing financing, negotiating talent contracts, managing budgets, and fostering collaboration among dozens of departments. He emphasized that a producer is “the connective tissue” that keeps a project afloat, constantly translating creative ideas into tangible deliverables. Weiss concluded the session with a Q&A that left attendees buzzing about how to navigate the volatile world of film production.

Bringing Baz Luhrmann’s “Joan of Arc” to Life

When Weiss joined Baz Luhrmann on the ambitious “Joan of Arc” project, he faced the challenge of marrying Luhrmann’s flamboyant visual style with a historically grounded narrative. Weiss described the process as a “tightrope walk” between art and commerce, noting that he had to negotiate with distributors who were wary of a period piece that could be perceived as niche. He highlighted the importance of early audience testing, securing a strong marketing partner, and maintaining a clear vision that resonated with both critics and mainstream viewers.

Karaoke, Jackman, and Hollywood Culture

In a lighter moment, Weiss recalled an impromptu karaoke session with Hugh Jackman during a break in the festival. The pair traded off songs from their own filmographies—Jackman belting “You’re So Vain” while Weiss improvised a rendition of “I’ll Be There.” According to Weiss, moments like these are essential for building trust on set, reminding everyone that behind the high‑stakes production lies a community of artists who enjoy each other’s company. The anecdote became a favorite talking point, illustrating that even in the world of blockbusters, camaraderie remains a cornerstone of success.
