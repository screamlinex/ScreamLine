SpaceX launches 28 Starlink satellites from California, lands rocket at sea

Summary: SpaceX successfully launched 28 new Starlink satellites on October 27 from California’s central coast, using its Falcon 9 rocket. The vehicle performed a flawless sea‑landing after the launch, marking another milestone for reusable launch technology.

Launch Details

The Falcon 9 rocket lifted off from Spaceport California at 10:15 a.m. PT, carrying a full payload of 28 Starlink V3 satellites. The launch vehicle followed the standard two‑stage ascent profile, reaching orbit after a 2‑minute 45‑second powered flight. The launch window was tightly constrained by orbital mechanics to place the satellites into their intended geostationary‑tangential orbits.

Satellite Payload

All 28 satellites belong to the next‑generation Starlink V3 constellation, featuring advanced phased‑array antennas and on‑board Ku‑band transponders. These new nodes will enhance global broadband coverage, reduce latency, and support emerging services such as low‑earth‑orbit (LEO) satellite‑based 5G backhaul. The payload mass was 3,120 kg, including the Starlink satellites, launch fairing, and the rocket’s upper stage.

Landing Operations

After separation, the first stage performed a controlled descent and landed on a maritime platform in the Pacific Ocean, approximately 200 km offshore. This sea‑landing, conducted by the Company’s Flight‑Termination System, demonstrates the continued reliability of the Falcon 9’s reusable first stage and reduces the need for costly on‑shore landing sites.

Future Outlook

With the successful deployment of 28 more Starlink V3 satellites, SpaceX moves closer to its target of 12,000 satellites in the constellation by the end of 2025. The company’s ability to land rockets at sea expands its launch flexibility, potentially enabling more frequent flights and new launch sites across the globe. For Starlink users, this expansion translates into broader coverage, lower latency, and improved service reliability.
