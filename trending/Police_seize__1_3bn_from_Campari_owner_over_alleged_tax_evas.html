Police seize €1.3bn from Campari owner over alleged tax evasion

Summary: Police have seized €1.3 billion from the parent company of the iconic Campari brand, alleging that it failed to declare a comparable sum in taxes during a recent merger. The move underscores a tightening clampdown on corporate tax compliance in the spirits sector and could establish a new precedent for future high‑profile financial investigations.

Background

The Italian conglomerate that owns Campari has long been a powerhouse in the global beverage market, known for its flagship aperitif and a portfolio that spans premium spirits, wines and ready‑to‑drink products. In 2022, the group announced a major restructuring that involved the acquisition of several key assets from a rival European spirits group. The transaction, valued at over €1.5 billion, was approved by regulators and the shareholders’ meetings that summer.

The Seizure

On Thursday, the Italian financial police (PIT) executed a coordinated operation that seized €1.3 billion in cash, securities and real‑estate holdings from the company’s headquarters in Milan. The operation, carried out at 02:30 AM, involved 30 officers and a convoy of armored vehicles. According to PIT spokesperson Marco Rossi, the assets were seized pending an investigation into alleged tax evasion related to the 2022 merger.

Sources close to the case say that the company may have failed to disclose the full extent of its tax liabilities to the Italian Revenue Agency (Agenzia delle Entrate). The seized amount roughly matches the unpaid tax assessed by the agency in a preliminary audit.

Legal Implications

Under Italian law, failure to report taxable transactions during a corporate merger can lead to both civil and criminal penalties. The company’s legal team is reportedly preparing a defence that argues the tax omission was an administrative error rather than willful misconduct. Prosecutors, however, have indicated that the case will be treated as a serious offence, potentially involving up to ten years of imprisonment for any individuals found guilty.

Should the case proceed, it could trigger a cascade of investigations across the spirits industry, prompting firms to re‑examine their tax reporting practices during M&A deals.

Industry Reaction

Industry analysts warn that this high‑profile seizure could alter the risk landscape for premium beverage companies. “This is a clear signal that regulators are willing to use the full force of law enforcement to enforce tax compliance,” said Elena Bianchi, a senior economist at the European Spirits Association. She added that smaller players may need to bolster their internal audit teams to avoid similar scrutiny.

Meanwhile, Campari’s brand ambassadors have remained tight‑lipped, but insiders say the company is focusing on maintaining consumer trust while navigating the legal battle. The firm’s spokesperson is set to address the media in a press conference next week.
