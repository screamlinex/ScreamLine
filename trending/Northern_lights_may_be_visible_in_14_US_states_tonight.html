Northern lights may be visible in 14 US states tonight

Summary: Auroras may be visible from Alaska to New York as geomagnetic storm conditions are predicted for tonight.

What Are Auroras?

When charged particles from the Sun collide with Earth's magnetic field, they excite atmospheric gases, producing luminous displays known as auroras. The northern hemisphere phenomenon—Aurora Borealis—appears as shimmering curtains of green, pink, and occasionally red light dancing across the night sky.

Geomagnetic Storm Outlook

NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory and NOAA’s Space Weather Prediction Center forecast a moderate geomagnetic storm tonight, with a Kp index of 4–5. This level of solar activity is sufficient for auroral displays to reach latitudes as low as 38°N, covering a wide swath of the United States from Alaska to the Northeast.

Which States Will See the Lights?

Observers in the following states could catch the aurora: Alaska, Washington, Oregon, Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, Colorado, Nebraska, Kansas, North Dakota, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, and New Jersey. The intensity will vary; higher latitudes will see brighter, more complex curtains, while lower latitudes may witness faint, diffuse glows.

Tips for Watching Tonight

Seek a dark, unobstructed horizon and allow your eyes to adapt for at least 15 minutes. A clear, moonless sky enhances visibility. Bring a tripod for a low-light camera if you want to capture the display. Remember to stay warm, as temperatures can drop dramatically during nighttime storm activity.
