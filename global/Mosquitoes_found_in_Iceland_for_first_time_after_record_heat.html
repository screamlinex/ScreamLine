Global News / October 22, 2025

Mosquitoes found in Iceland for first time after record heat

Summary: For the first time in its history, Iceland is dealing with an influx of mosquitoes, a direct consequence of a record-breaking heatwave that has altered the island’s climate patterns.

The Unexpected Invasion

Wildlife officials in Reykjavik confirmed the presence of Aedes aegypti and Culex pipiens mosquitoes in the capital’s outskirts, a species previously absent from the island’s cool, dry environment. The insects were first spotted in late July, after a 15‑day streak of temperatures exceeding 30 °C—an unprecedented high for the region.

Why Iceland Is a Mosquito Haven Now

Scientists attribute the sudden emergence to a combination of warming air, increased humidity, and stagnant water bodies created by heavy rainfall. The new conditions provide the ideal breeding ground for mosquitoes that thrive in warmer climates.

Impact on Tourism and Local Life

Tourists who flock to the Blue Lagoon and Golden Circle are reporting itchy bites and a sudden shift in the island’s usually serene atmosphere. Local residents are scrambling to install mosquito nets and purchase repellents, while city councils are launching public awareness campaigns.

What Scientists Are Saying

Dr. Elín Þórsdóttir, a climatologist at the University of Iceland, warns that if global temperatures continue to climb, “Iceland could become a year‑round hotspot for these vectors, raising the risk of diseases such as West Nile virus.” She urges immediate action to monitor mosquito populations and implement control measures.
