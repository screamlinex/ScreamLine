Global News / October 28, 2025

Watch: Storm chasers fly inside the eye of Hurricane Melissa

Summary: Footage taken by a U.S. Air Force Reserve crew known as the "Hurricane Hunters" shows Hurricane Melissa—a Category 5 storm—intensifying in the Caribbean, revealing swirling winds and thunderous rain that have stunned both scientists and locals.

Inside the Storm

The most striking moments come from the plane's interior, where pilots and meteorologists watched the eye's calm center and the violent eyewall in real time. Cameras captured swirling bands of dark cloud, the razor‑sharp wind shear, and the eerie silence that suddenly erupts into thunder when the plane crosses into the outer rain bands.

Analysts note that the footage confirms Melissa’s rapid intensification, a phenomenon that can be traced back to unusually warm ocean temperatures and low wind shear in the Caribbean basin.

The Hurricane Hunters

The U.S. Air Force Reserve’s 53rd Weather Reconnaissance Squadron, affectionately called the "Hurricane Hunters," has flown into Melissa’s eye three times during the storm’s lifecycle. Their data—pressure readings, wind speeds, and atmospheric profiles—helps refine predictive models and improve early‑warning systems for coastal communities.

In this latest sortie, the crew recorded wind speeds exceeding 170 mph inside the eyewall, a figure that aligns with the storm’s Category 5 status and underscores the severity of the threat.

Impact and Response

Caribbean nations have issued emergency alerts and begun evacuations in the storm’s projected path. Local officials are coordinating with international aid agencies to prepare shelters and secure critical infrastructure.

Despite the intensity, the immediate damage footprint has been limited, thanks largely to the early detection and swift response facilitated by the Hurricane Hunters’ data.

Future Outlook

Forecast models predict that Melissa will maintain its Category 5 status as it approaches the Lesser Antilles before gradually weakening over cooler waters. Meteorologists emphasize the importance of continuous monitoring, as even slight changes in sea‑surface temperature can alter the storm’s trajectory.

For now, the world watches the relentless power of nature, while scientists and emergency responders stay on standby to protect lives and property.
