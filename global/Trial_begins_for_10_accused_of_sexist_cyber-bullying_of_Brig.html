Global News / October 27, 2025

Trial begins for 10 accused of sexist cyber-bullying of Brigitte Macron

Summary: The trial has opened in Paris, where ten individuals face charges for allegedly disseminating sexist, misogynistic online content that questioned Brigitte Macron’s gender and sexual orientation.

Background of the Allegations

In early 2024, a series of anonymous social‑media posts circulated on platforms such as Twitter and TikTok, claiming that France’s first lady, Brigitte Macron, was not biologically female and was in a same‑sex relationship. The posts were accompanied by graphic memes and sensational headlines that quickly went viral, sparking widespread outrage and condemnation across the political spectrum.

Investigators traced the origin of the content to a network of ten individuals who coordinated the dissemination through private chat groups and public forums. The defendants are alleged to have fabricated the claims in an attempt to undermine the Macron family's reputation.

The Trial Begins

The proceedings started on Monday at the Paris Court of First Instance, with the prosecution presenting digital evidence, including screenshots, IP logs, and testimonies from social‑media platform moderators. The defense has argued that the defendants were merely exercising “free expression” and that the claims were satirical.

Both sides have called on witnesses who were part of the online communities involved, while a forensic analyst has highlighted the use of VPNs and anonymizing services that complicate attribution.

Public Reaction and Political Fallout

The announcement of the trial has reignited debates about online harassment, gender politics, and the limits of free speech in France. Members of Parliament from both sides of the aisle have urged swift action, citing the need to protect public figures from “digital hate.”

In a televised address, President Emmanuel Macron called the allegations “unacceptable” and emphasized that the judiciary’s role is to ensure justice, not to stifle debate.

Legal Context

France’s cyber‑bullying laws have recently been strengthened, allowing for criminal penalties for the intentional spread of defamatory or hateful content. The court will consider whether the defendants’ actions meet the statutory threshold of “mens rea” (intent) for a cyber‑harassment offence.

Experts point out that this case could set a precedent for how the judiciary interprets “defamation” in the age of social media, potentially influencing future prosecutions of similar content.

Looking Ahead

With the trial scheduled to run for six weeks, legal analysts predict that the proceedings will be closely monitored by media watchdogs and civil‑rights groups. The outcome could either reinforce France’s commitment to combating online misogyny or raise concerns about chilling effects on free expression.

Meanwhile, the Macrons have urged the public to remain calm, emphasizing that the legal process will ultimately determine the truth behind the allegations.
