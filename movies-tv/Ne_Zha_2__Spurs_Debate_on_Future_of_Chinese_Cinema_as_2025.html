‘Ne Zha 2’ Spurs Debate on Future of Chinese Cinema as 2025 Blue Book Launches at Tokyo Market

Summary: At the Tokyo International Film Festival’s TIFFCOM market, the launch of the Blue Book of China Film 2025—jointly edited by Peking University’s Chen Xuguang and Zhejiang University’s Fan Zhizhong—framed Chinese cinema at a crossroads between crisis and renewal. According to the report, annual box office revenue in 2024 fell 22.6% year‑on‑year, while the domestic streaming market grew by 15%, prompting a debate about the future of Chinese filmmaking in a rapidly digitalising industry. The Blue Book’s release coincided with the highly‑anticipated premiere of ‘Ne Zha 2’, which, despite modest domestic performance, sparked discussion on the viability of sequel‑centric, high‑budget animation in China’s competitive film landscape.

Blue Book 2025: A Prognostic Lens on Chinese Film

The Blue Book offers a comprehensive audit of the Chinese film ecosystem, charting trends from box office declines to the surge of overseas co‑productions. Its authors highlight a 22.6% drop in 2024 domestic receipts, attributing the slump to lingering pandemic effects, rising ticket prices, and shifting audience habits toward streaming platforms. Yet the report is not purely a lamentation; it also showcases the resilience of Chinese studios that have pivoted to hybrid distribution models, blending theatrical releases with digital premieres to broaden reach.

Ne Zha 2: A Case Study in Sequel Strategy

‘Ne Zha 2’, the sequel to the 2019 blockbuster that revived China’s animation sector, opened at #3 in its first weekend, grossing $12 million—well below the $30 million benchmark of the original. Analysts argue that the film’s failure to recapture the first film’s magic stems from a diluted narrative and a rushed production schedule, reflecting a broader trend of studios prioritising speed over substance. Despite the modest box office, the film’s strong online viewership—surpassing 150 million streams in the first month—demonstrates the growing importance of digital platforms for animation titles.

Industry Reactions: From Crisis to Opportunity

Industry insiders view the Blue Book’s findings as a call to action. Some executives are leaning into international co‑production to spread risk and tap into global talent pools, while others advocate for a renewed focus on original, culturally resonant stories that can compete both domestically and abroad. The report’s recommendation for a “dual‑channel” model—simultaneous theatrical and streaming releases—has already seen early adoption by studios like Shanghai Animation Film Studio, which announced a joint release strategy for its upcoming 2025 projects.

Looking Ahead: Policy, Innovation, and Global Ambitions

In response to the Blue Book’s insights, the Ministry of Culture is reportedly drafting new incentives for green‑energy film production and digital‑first releases. Meanwhile, Beijing’s “Made in China 2025” initiative is pushing studios to invest in AI‑driven CGI and immersive storytelling technologies, hoping to create globally competitive blockbusters that can rival Hollywood’s visual spectacle. The coming years will test whether Chinese cinema can navigate this “crossroads” and emerge with a diversified, resilient creative economy.
