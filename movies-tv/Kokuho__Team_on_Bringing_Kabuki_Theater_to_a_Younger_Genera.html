‘Kokuho’ Team on Bringing Kabuki Theater to a Younger Generation: ‘It’s a Movie About Art, Two Actors and the Life of Actors’

Summary: In a candid roundtable hosted by Variety on Oct. 26 in Los Angeles, production designer Yohei Taneda and producer Chieko Murata joined co‑editor‑in‑chief Cynthia Littleton to discuss the Japanese drama “Kokuho.” The conversation unpacked how the film blends traditional Kabuki theater with contemporary storytelling, focusing on the lives of two actors and the broader art of performance.

Bridging Tradition and Youth: The Heart of “Kokuho”

“Kokuho” is more than a biopic of Kabuki; it’s a meditation on the living art form that has captivated Japanese audiences for centuries. Murata explained that the drama seeks to make Kabuki accessible to younger viewers by weaving the historical grandeur of the stage with modern character arcs, allowing audiences to see the performers as relatable, multifaceted individuals.

Designing Authenticity: Yohei Taneda’s Vision

Yohei Taneda emphasized the importance of authenticity in every set piece. “We wanted the audience to feel the weight of a Kabuki stage without the usual theatrical excess,” Taneda said. He described collaborating closely with costume designers and set architects to recreate the iconic gold‑leafed backdrops while incorporating subtle digital effects to highlight the emotional depth of the actors’ journeys.

Producer Perspective: Chieko Murata on Bringing the Drama to the Screen

Murata opened the discussion by outlining her mission: “Kabuki offers a unique lens into Japanese culture, but it can feel distant to modern viewers.” She recounted how the production team used a dual narrative—one following the seasoned Kabuki master, the other a rising star—to illustrate the generational bridge. “The drama is a love letter to art, to the actors, and to the life they live,” she said.

Cynthia Littleton on the Global Appeal of Kabuki

Littleton highlighted the film’s potential to resonate beyond Japan. “Kabuki’s stylized gestures and storytelling have a universal language,” she noted. She praised the filmmakers’ ability to balance cultural specificity with themes of ambition, identity, and resilience that speak to any audience.

Audience Reception: A New Generation Embracing the Stage

Early screenings in Los Angeles have already sparked conversations on social media, with viewers praising the film’s vivid portrayal of Kabuki and its relatable characters. Critics point out that “Kokuho” successfully demystifies a traditionally elite art form, inviting a broader, younger demographic into the fold.
