Kendrick Lamar’s ‘Luther’ Departs Hot 100, Leaving No Rap Songs in the Top 40 for First Time in 35 Years

Summary: “Luther,” the SZA‑featuring single by Kendrick Lamar, held the number‑one spot on the Billboard Hot 100 for 13 consecutive weeks before dropping out of the Top 40. For the first time in 35 years, no rap tracks appear inside the Top 40, a shift linked to recent chart‑eligibility rule changes and evolving streaming patterns.

Chart Performance of “Luther”

Debuting at number 73, “Luther” surged to the top of the Hot 100 in its fifth week and maintained the #1 position for 13 weeks. The single’s cumulative 35‑week run on the chart underscores its lasting appeal, yet its exit from the Top 40 marks a new low for rap presence in that echelon.

Billboard Eligibility Rule Changes

In 2024, Billboard updated its chart methodology to exclude certain streaming services and to require a higher proportion of paid streams for songs to chart. These adjustments disproportionately affect hip‑hop tracks, which often rely on streaming from platforms that were previously counted fully. The new rules also tightened the definition of “mainstream radio airplay,” limiting the exposure of rap songs that do not cross over to pop‑friendly stations.

Industry Response

Artists and label executives have voiced concern over the altered criteria. “The changes favor pop over rap in ways that don’t reflect listener habits,” said a senior VP at a major hip‑hop label. Analysts predict a temporary decline in rap representation on the Hot 100, though they expect the genre to rebound as streaming platforms adapt and new releases surface.

Implications for Rap Visibility

The absence of rap songs from the Top 40 affects radio programming, playlist curation, and artist promotion strategies. With fewer rap tracks receiving high‑profile placements, emerging artists may face greater challenges in gaining mainstream exposure. The industry may need to pivot toward alternative metrics, such as social‑media engagement and niche streaming data, to better capture rap’s cultural influence.
