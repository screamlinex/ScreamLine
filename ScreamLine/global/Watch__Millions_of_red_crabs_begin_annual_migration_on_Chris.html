Watch: Millions of red crabs begin annual migration on Christmas Island

Summary: The island off mainland Australia experiences the event every summer, when around 50 million crabs scuttle down from their homes to the beach to lay eggs.

Seasonal Spectacle

Each year, from late June to early July, the red crabs emerge from the rainforest and make their way to the shore in a procession that can last for weeks. The sight of millions of bright orange shells marching across the sand is one of the most dramatic natural events on the planet.

Massive Numbers

Scientists estimate that up to 50 million crabs participate in the migration, making it the largest known terrestrial animal migration in the world. The sheer volume of these creatures can temporarily block roads and even alter local traffic patterns.

Impact on the Ecosystem

When the crabs arrive at the beach, they begin to lay eggs in the sand. The resulting egg beds provide a rich food source for a variety of shorebirds and other wildlife, while the crabs themselves help aerate the soil and recycle nutrients back into the forest ecosystem.

Tourist Attraction and Conservation Efforts

The migration draws thousands of visitors each year, boosting the local economy but also raising concerns about human impact. Local authorities have implemented measures such as designated viewing platforms, strict no‑touch policies, and educational programs to protect both the crabs and their habitat while allowing people to witness the phenomenon safely.
