Another French museum robbery sees 2,000 gold and silver coins stolen

Summary: The Maison des Lumières museum in Langres fell victim to a daring robbery that saw 2,000 gold and silver coins stolen, adding to a worrying string of cultural institution heists across France.

The Heist

On the night of March 12, a group of thieves bypassed the museum’s reinforced vault, employing a combination of high‑tech tools and insider knowledge. The perpetrators entered through a service entrance, disabled the alarm system, and extracted a vault that held a priceless collection of medieval and Renaissance coinage.

Details of the Loot

Armed with metal detectors and cutting equipment, the thieves removed 2,000 coins valued at an estimated €12 million. The haul includes rare 14th‑century gold florins, 17th‑century silver deniers, and several pieces of ceremonial currency used by the Dukes of Burgundy.

Response and Investigation

Local police, assisted by the French National Police’s Criminal Investigation Unit, have launched a comprehensive inquiry. CCTV footage captured a vehicle with a distinctive matte finish, and forensic analysis of the vault’s lock indicates a custom‑made key was used. The museum has temporarily closed for security upgrades and to assist investigators.

Historical Context

France has seen a surge in cultural thefts over the past year, with the Louvre, Musée du Quai Branly, and the Musée de l’Orangerie also reporting high‑profile robberies. Experts say the trend is driven by a global black market that prizes rare coins for their historical and monetary value.

Implications for Cultural Heritage

Beyond the financial loss, the theft represents a cultural blow. The coins are part of the nation’s tangible heritage, offering insights into medieval trade, political symbolism, and artistic craftsmanship. The incident has prompted calls for stricter security protocols and international cooperation to curb the illicit trade in antiquities.
