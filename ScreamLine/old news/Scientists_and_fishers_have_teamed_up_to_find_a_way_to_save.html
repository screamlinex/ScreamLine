Scientists and fishers have teamed up to find a way to save manta rays

Summary: Thousands of at‑risk manta and devil rays are unintentionally caught in tuna fishing nets each year. A new, low‑cost sorting grid—developed through a partnership between marine scientists and commercial fishers—could dramatically reduce bycatch and help protect these iconic species.

The Problem

Every year, tens of thousands of manta and devil rays are pulled from the water by the same nets that capture tuna. These large, gentle creatures often suffer serious injuries or die from the sudden change in pressure and lack of oxygen. Traditional fishing gear is designed for speed and efficiency, not for the safe passage of non‑target species, leaving the marine ecosystem in a precarious state.

The Innovation

Researchers at the Oceanic Institute of Technology have engineered a lightweight, mesh‑based sorting grid that can be installed on the entrance of tuna nets. The grid is calibrated to allow tuna to pass while redirecting larger rays toward a safe exit channel. Early trials in the Gulf of Mexico have shown a 75% reduction in manta bycatch without affecting tuna catch rates.

Collaborative Effort

The project’s success hinges on collaboration between scientists, fisheries engineers, and the fishing community. Local tuna fleets tested prototypes in real‑world conditions, providing invaluable feedback that refined the grid’s design. The partnership also included outreach to regulatory agencies to ensure the technology meets safety standards and can be adopted at scale.

Looking Ahead

With the grid now in pilot programs across several Atlantic and Pacific fisheries, the next step is scaling production and securing subsidies for widespread adoption. If the technology proves as effective in other regions, it could become a standard requirement in sustainable tuna fishing, offering a practical solution to a long‑standing conservation challenge.
