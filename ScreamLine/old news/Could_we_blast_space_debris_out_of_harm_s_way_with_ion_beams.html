Could we blast space debris out of harm's way with ion beams?

Summary: A new space debris removal project, ALBATOR, plans to tackle the growing debris problem in orbit.

What is ALBATOR?

ALBATOR (Advanced Laser-Based Debris Avoidance and Targeting Operations) is an international consortium of space agencies and private firms that has announced a bold plan to use high‑energy ion beams to nudge hazardous debris out of the crowded orbits that house the International Space Station and other critical payloads. The project aims to demonstrate a scalable, non‑kinetic method of debris mitigation that could complement existing removal techniques such as drag augmentation or robotic grappling.

How Ion Beams Work

Unlike conventional lasers that rely on photon pressure, ion beam technology accelerates charged particles to near‑relativistic speeds and directs them at target objects. When the ions collide with a piece of debris, they deposit momentum and heat, causing a small but measurable change in trajectory. Because the ions can be pulsed in precise bursts, the system can be tuned to adjust the orbit of a debris fragment without damaging surrounding satellites.

Mission Design and Deployment

ALBATOR’s first test mission will launch a dedicated ion‑beam platform on a small satellite bus into a low‑Earth orbit. The platform will carry a 10‑kilowatt ion accelerator and a suite of sensors to track debris positions in real time. In partnership with ESA’s Space Debris Office, the system will target a catalogued 30‑centimeter fragment that poses a collision risk to the ISS. By the end of the 12‑month trial, the consortium expects to demonstrate a measurable orbital adjustment of the debris and validate the beam‑tracking algorithms.

Challenges and Outlook

Despite its promise, ion‑beam debris removal faces significant hurdles. Precise beam pointing requires extremely stable platforms, and the energy demands of accelerating ions to high velocities are non‑trivial for small satellites. There is also the risk of creating secondary debris if the ion impact spawns micro‑particles. Nonetheless, proponents argue that the technology offers a rapid, low‑mass alternative to current methods, and that continued research could unlock a new class of on‑orbit debris mitigation tools.
