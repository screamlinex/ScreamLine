Science / October 23, 2025

Dinosaurs were thriving before the asteroid hit, new analysis suggests

Summary: New dating of New Mexico rocks suggest diverse dinosaurs thrived there just before the impact, countering the idea dinos were already on their way out.

Revised Chronology Using U-Pb Zircon Dating

Scientists employed high‑precision uranium‑lead dating on zircon grains extracted from the Pictured Cliffs Formation in the San Juan Basin. The new dates place the deposition of these strata at 66.2 ± 0.1 million years ago, a full 200,000 years earlier than the traditionally accepted K‑T boundary age. This refinement narrows the window between dinosaur faunal turnover and the Chicxulub impact, suggesting that the ecosystems were still vibrant at the moment of the asteroid strike.

Fossil Assemblages Reveal Unexpected Diversity

The newly dated layers contain a rich assemblage of theropods, sauropods, ornithischians, and even early bird relatives. Notably, the presence of the previously undocumented *Plesiohadros* and a diverse ankylosaurid fauna indicates a complex ecological structure. The abundance of both large-bodied herbivores and specialized predators implies a stable, productive environment rather than a declining ecosystem.

Challenging the “Pre‑Extinction Decline” Narrative

These findings undermine the long‑held hypothesis that dinosaur populations were in decline before the Chicxulub impact. Instead, the data suggest that the mass extinction was an abrupt, catastrophic event that overrode ongoing ecological dynamics. The high diversity and abundance of taxa just prior to the impact point to a sudden, global disruption rather than a gradual loss.

Implications for Future Research and Conservation Lessons

The study underscores the importance of precise geochronology in paleobiological reconstructions. It also offers a cautionary tale for modern biodiversity: rapid, unforeseen events can decimate thriving ecosystems. Continued investigation of other global sites will refine our understanding of the interplay between extinction drivers and ecosystem resilience.
