Big questions on how food affects our health

Summary: Editor in Chief Nancy Shute explores the science behind major questions on food and health — from the addictive potential of ultraprocessed foods to the high‑protein diet craze to the drawbacks of keto.

The Allure and Addictive Nature of Ultra‑Processed Foods

Ultra‑processed items, from sugary cereals to flavored chips, are engineered to trigger dopamine release through a precise mix of fats, sugars, and salt. Recent neuroimaging studies show that the brain’s reward circuitry reacts as strongly to these foods as it does to drugs of abuse, which explains why many people struggle to resist them. However, the same studies also reveal that the brain adapts over time, requiring larger doses for the same reward — a classic pattern of tolerance and withdrawal that fuels chronic overeating and weight gain.

High‑Protein Diets: Benefits, Risks, and the Science

High‑protein regimens have surged in popularity, touted for muscle building and fat loss. Meta‑analyses confirm modest short‑term weight loss and increased satiety, but long‑term data are mixed. Excess protein, especially from animal sources, can raise the risk of kidney strain, osteoporosis, and certain cancers, while plant‑based proteins offer a lower risk profile. The key, experts suggest, is balance: adequate protein to support muscle and metabolism, paired with a diverse intake of fiber, vitamins, and healthy fats.

The Keto Craze: What the Research Says

Ketogenic diets, characterized by high fat and very low carbohydrate intake, have shown promise for seizure control, type‑2 diabetes management, and rapid weight loss. Yet, the long‑term sustainability and cardiovascular effects remain uncertain. Studies indicate that while some individuals experience improved insulin sensitivity, others see increased LDL cholesterol and liver strain. Moreover, the restrictive nature of keto can lead to nutrient deficiencies, making it essential to monitor micronutrient status and tailor the diet to individual health goals.

Balancing Nutrient Intake for Long‑Term Health

Emerging evidence supports a flexible, whole‑food approach that prioritizes plant‑based staples, moderate animal proteins, and healthy fats, while limiting refined sugars and ultra‑processed items. Personalized nutrition, guided by genetic markers, gut microbiome profiling, and metabolic markers, is becoming increasingly feasible. Ultimately, the most sustainable diets are those that fit an individual’s lifestyle, preferences, and health objectives, ensuring long‑term adherence and overall well‑being.
